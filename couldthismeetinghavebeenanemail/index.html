Could This Meeting Have Been an Email?

The definitive assessment tool for the modern workplace

Question 1 of 10
1. How many people are invited to this meeting?
2. What's the primary purpose of this meeting?
3. How likely is it that this topic will generate debate or discussion?
4. Does the meeting organizer have a clear agenda?
5. How emotionally charged is the subject matter?
6. How many visual aids or demonstrations are needed?
7. What's the real reason this meeting was called?
8. How many times has someone said "I'll circle back" in previous meetings on this topic?
9. How quickly do you need responses or decisions?
10. Be honest: How many people will be secretly checking emails during this meeting?
This Could Have Been an Email

Save everyone's time and sanity by crafting a well-written email instead. Your colleagues will silently thank you by actually reading it, unlike the meeting where they'd be checking their emails anyway.

Pro tip: Use bullet points, keep it concise, and if you need responses, clearly mark action items and deadlines.

Well, That Depends...

This is in the gray area. Consider a hybrid approach: send a detailed email first, then hold an optional 15-minute meeting for questions and discussion.

Alternatively, use a collaborative document or chat thread that allows for asynchronous input while respecting everyone's time.

Yes, This Needs to Be a Meeting

Some things genuinely require real-time discussion and collaboration. But please, still send an agenda, keep it focused, and end early if possible.

Remember: A good meeting leaves everyone feeling their time was well spent, not wondering why they weren't just cc'd on an email.

No meetings were harmed in the making of this assessment. If you've actually used this to make a decision, we're both impressed and concerned.
